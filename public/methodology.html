Methodology

CivicLens reconstructs what members of Congress did — trades, votes, sponsorships, and donors — from primary sources, then runs a fixed set of deterministic detectors over that record. This page documents how each detector measures a pattern, how confidence is computed, and what the numbers can and cannot show. It reports patterns, not verdicts.

How a flag is scored

  • A flag is a measured pattern in the public record — never an accusation or a finding of wrongdoing.
  • Confidence comes from a permutation null model. For a scored detector, the observed count is compared against many shuffles of the member's own activity, holding their pace fixed. The p-value is the share of shuffles that match or exceed the observed count; a low p-value means the pattern is hard to produce by that member's ordinary behaviour alone.
  • Statistics are shown, never invented. Detectors that are not scored against a null model say so plainly and show only the count of cited records — no fabricated p-value.
  • Weight reflects evidence strength, not a verdict. Visual intensity on a flag card maps to the strength of the statistical signal; it carries no moral colour.
  • Everything is reproducible. The null model is seeded, so the same inputs yield the same scores. Every flag cites real rows you can verify in the sections above it.

Detectors

The deterministic detection pass. Each runs over the corpus and only emits a flag when it can cite real underlying rows.

Trade ↔ vote alignment
trade-vote-alignment
How measured: Counts discretionary stock trades placed within 14 days before a vote on a bill the member's committee handled or whose text names the traded company, then compares that count to a seeded permutation null built from the member's own trading pace.
What this doesn't show: Timing proximity is not proof of intent or of non-public information. Committee jurisdiction is a proxy for influence, not evidence of a hearing on that company.
Spousal trade timing
spousal-trade-timing
How measured: Flags trades held by a spouse or jointly, placed within 14 days before a vote on a bill the member's committee handled.
What this doesn't show: Spouse or joint accounts may be independently managed. CivicLens has no hearing calendar; committee jurisdiction is the relevance proxy.
Donor sector ↔ sponsorship
donor-sector-vote-alignment
How measured: Flags economic sectors where the member both draws top campaign money and personally sponsors focused legislation, using a hand-curated, version-controlled industry-to-theme crosswalk.
What this doesn't show: Drawing donations from, and legislating on, the same sector is expected for members who represent that industry. This is alignment, not a quid pro quo.

Detectors we tested and rejected

A detector only ships if it can tell the real world from a shuffled one. Each candidate detector is pre-registered — statistic, null model, thresholds, and random seed fixed in code before the test runs — then run once against its null. If it fails, it is not published, not re-tuned, and not re-run. The failures are documented here with the same weight as the successes, because a platform that can only confirm its own hypotheses is an accusation engine, not a measurement instrument.

District contracts ↔ trades (industry level)
rejected 2026-07-16 · p = 0.48
Hypothesis: members trade the industries that receive federal contract dollars in their own district. Tested by mapping USAspending contract dollars (33 House districts, CY2023–25) to industry themes and comparing each member's trades against their district's contract mix, with a member↔district shuffle as the null.
Why it failed: defense and aerospace dominate the contract mix of nearly every district, and congressional portfolios hold the same large caps — so shuffled member↔district pairs "matched" as often as real ones (p = 0.48). Industry-level matching carries no district-specific information.
District contracts ↔ trades (exact company)
rejected 2026-07-19 · p = 0.74 / 0.38
Hypothesis: members trade the specific companies receiving federal contracts performed in their district. Tested by resolving 57,199 district contract recipients to stock tickers via SAM.gov corporate hierarchy and SEC issuer records — 397 recipient→ticker links hand-confirmed under a curation protocol blinded to members, districts, dollars, and outcomes — then comparing overlap breadth and dollar exposure against a 2,000-permutation member↔district shuffle. Tickers receiving contracts in over a third of districts were excluded up front as uninformative. A negative control (scrambled ticker identities, 20 replicates) returned 0 false positives, confirming the instrument works.
Why it failed: observed overlap was indistinguishable from — on the breadth measure, below — what random district assignment produces (p = 0.74 breadth, p = 0.38 dollars). Statistical power was limited: only 9 of 33 harvested members traded individual stocks in the window. The full audit trail — blind worklist, every accepted and rejected identity link, the pre-registered analysis code, and the run transcript — is in the public repository.

Neither result proves the absence of a relationship between congressional trading and federal contracting. Each means one specific, honestly-tested method found nothing stronger than chance — and was retired instead of adjusted until it "worked."
